## 1. Executive Summary

The romantic story that profitable trading hypotheses mainly come from brilliant unconstrained ideation is mostly wrong. The strongest public evidence points instead to a small set of recurring edge families: trend/time-series momentum, value, carry, post-earnings underreaction, bond term premia, hedging-pressure and term-structure effects in commodities, some relative-value/stat-arb structures, and volatility/insurance premia. Those edges tend to work when they are tied to persistent frictions such as slow information diffusion, risk transfer, funding/liquidity constraints, market structure, or durable demand for convexity/insurance. They tend to decay when publication, crowding, structural change, or implementation costs overwhelm the friction.

That has a sharp implication for BoxRoomCapital: do **not** build around a free-form "AI idea factory." Build around an approved taxonomy of edge mechanisms, hard falsification gates, and cost-aware validation. Publication effects, multiple testing, and backtest overfitting are not side problems; they are the main disease. Out-of-sample returns in published anomalies fall materially, the old t-stat > 2 convention is too weak in a factor zoo, high in-sample Sharpe can be meaningless, and combining the "best few" signals out of many dramatically worsens data-mining risk.

The best public descriptions of current high-end teams sound much less like genius trading salons and much more like scientific organizations. Man AHL describes a team of researchers, developers, and traders applying scientific rigor and robust technology across hundreds of markets. Bridgewater emphasizes a research process built around accurate understanding of reality and an idea-meritocratic challenge culture. Two Sigma emphasizes falsifiability, hidden out-samples, MVPs, and treating negative results as progress. That is the right template. It also means your starting ambition--"one-person automated hedge fund" across global markets--is too wide for an MVP if the goal is robustness rather than cosplay.

AI is genuinely useful, but mainly as leverage on coverage, synthesis, literature retrieval, structured challenge, coding assistance, and live monitoring. It is dangerous as an autonomous PM, an unconstrained hypothesis generator, or a parliament of eloquent bots voting themselves into delusion. Practitioners and regulators are aligned on the hazards: agentic systems can amplify multiple testing, contaminate historical evaluation through pretrained knowledge, create explainability and data-integrity problems, increase vendor concentration and correlated-position risk, and in more autonomous settings may take actions that human managers do not properly understand. RAG, multi-model cross-checks, and modular workflows help, but they do not turn probabilistic language models into trustworthy capital allocators.

My bottom-line recommendation is narrower than your initial framing. The optimal design is not your initial four-character cast. It is a stage-gated research and deployment system with specialized agents for provenance, state/regime, hypothesis framing, mechanism review, falsification, experiment registration, cost-realistic testing, portfolio expression, model risk, and live retirement--plus a human CIO/risk owner who retains deployment authority. In MVP form, start in a small set of liquid instruments and 1-2 edge families with strong prior evidence; treat options mainly as an expression layer; postpone OTC credit, vol-specialist strategies, and crypto autonomy until the process proves it can reject more bad ideas than it produces.

## 2. Historical Review of Trading Hypothesis Generation

Historically, profitable hypothesis generation has been less about inventing novelty and more about noticing repeatable frictions, then converting them into tradeable rules or playbooks. The reusable mechanism families are: slow information incorporation and expectation revision; risk transfer and carry; funding/liquidity constraints and limits to arbitrage; inventory/hedging pressure and forced flow; relative-value dislocations; and persistent demand for convexity/insurance. This matters because it suggests BoxRoomCapital should organize idea generation around **edge families** rather than around vibes, stylistic personas, or whatever the robot dreamed up after three espressos.

**Discretionary macro -- strong practitioner tradition, mixed public alpha evidence.** Historically, macro ideas came from forming views on growth, inflation, policy, capital flows, and price/fundamental dislocations across rates, FX, equities, and commodities. The clean public evidence is stronger for the systematic pieces than for star-manager mythology: carry predicts returns across global equities, bonds, commodities, Treasuries, credit, and options; forward-rate structure predicts bond excess returns; and integrated macro frameworks combine systematic fundamental-trend components with opportunistic dislocation trades. The exploited edge is slow repricing of macro conditions plus persistent risk transfer; the failure modes are classic macro sins--narrative overfitting, carry crashes, and regime shifts that make yesterday's "timeless" playbook look like a museum exhibit.

**Fundamental research / variant perception -- strong practitioner logic, strongest public evidence around revisions and underreaction.** Classical fundamental hypothesis generation has always been about the gap between one's view and the market's embedded expectations, plus a catalyst that forces convergence. The empirical anchor is post-earnings-announcement drift: stock prices continue moving in the direction of earnings surprises rather than fully adjusting immediately. The useful lesson is that "good company analysis" is not enough; the edge comes from anticipating revisions in consensus expectations, not merely knowing the company better than the next person at the conference buffet. It persists because information diffusion, attention, and institutional incentives are slow; it decays when coverage broadens and faster processing compresses the revision window.

**Technical / trend following -- strong empirical evidence.** The hypothesis-generation mechanism is simple: recent price direction contains information about future returns because markets underreact, capital reallocates slowly, and trend-chasing or risk-management flows reinforce moves. Time-series momentum has been profitable on average across major futures since 1985, and long-run evidence extends a basic trend-following strategy back to 1880, with consistent profitability over the next 110 years. This is not evidence that chart doodles are magic; it is evidence that continuation is a durable market phenomenon. Persistence likely comes from behavioral underreaction and flow dynamics; the main decay mode is not disappearance but crash risk during sharp reversals and panic/rebound states.

**Event-driven -- moderate to strong evidence in specific niches.** Event-driven hypothesis generation typically starts with a discrete corporate event that creates a spread between current price and an expected outcome distribution--mergers, restructurings, bankruptcies, index changes, capital actions, litigation, regulatory decisions. Merger arbitrage is the cleanest example: after announcement, arbitrageurs provide liquidity by buying target shares at a discount to the offer, earning excess returns as compensation for nonlinear deal-break risk. The edge persists because legal/regulatory analysis, deal-specific probability assessment, and balance-sheet commitment are specialized. It fails when financing conditions tighten, deals break, or risk is misread as "free carry."

**Stat arb / relative value -- real but implementation-heavy.** Relative-value ideas historically emerged from law-of-one-price violations or temporary divergences among close substitutes. Classic pairs trading showed average annualized excess returns up to 11% over 1962-2002, above conservative transaction-cost estimates; newer evidence still finds positive returns over the last twenty years, though lower and more factor-sensitive. The economic story is temporary mispricing plus limits to arbitrage. The decay story is equally important: faster data, tighter spreads, more crowding, and higher implementation sensitivity mean that whatever survives today is narrower and far more execution-dependent than old academic backtests suggest.

**Factor/style investing across equities, rates, FX, commodities, and credit -- the strongest cross-asset evidence base.** The durable mechanism here is not "discover a new factor every Tuesday"; it is harvesting a small family of styles that reappear in different guises across markets. Value and momentum show up across eight diverse markets and asset classes and are negatively correlated with each other. Carry predicts returns in global equities, bonds, Treasuries, commodities, credit, and options. In fixed income and credit, value, momentum, carry, and defensive styles show evidence of positive risk-adjusted performance; in commodities, hedging pressure and term structure matter; in FX, carry works but carries crash risk in bad states. These edges persist because hedgers, insurers, borrowers, and levered investors continually transfer risk; they decay when capital crowds the style or when implementation friction dominates paper returns.

This is also where the graveyard of bad research gets noisy. Post-publication returns fall sharply; traditional significance thresholds are too lenient; and selecting the "best k out of n" candidate signals can create bias almost as bad as searching over an exponentially larger strategy set. The historical lesson is brutal and useful: old, boring, implementable styles deserve a much higher prior than glossy novelty with suspiciously pristine backtests.

**Options / volatility trading -- strong evidence for premia, but much of it is disguised insurance warehousing.** Hypothesis generation in options and vol has historically started from a mismatch between the price of convexity and the realized distribution of shocks. Variance risk premium can be measured by comparing realized variance with option-implied variance; skew premium explains a large share of the slope of the SPX vol surface. The edge here often comes from persistent demand for crash protection or event-specific convexity. That is why it can persist. It is also why it can be treacherous: much apparent "alpha" is simply being paid to absorb disaster risk. For an MVP, options should usually be treated as an expression layer for validated underlying theses, not as the first hunting ground for autonomous idea generation.

**Crypto -- moderate empirical evidence, very high operational and structural risk.** The cleanest public evidence is in basis/carry and perpetual-futures mechanics, not in narrative token-picking. BIS research shows crypto carry is poorly explained by ordinary interest differentials and is better understood through smaller trend-following investor demand interacting with limits to arbitrage created by regulation, margining frictions, and venue segmentation. Separate work on perpetual futures finds that basis, momentum, volume, size, and volatility-related predictors matter, and some arbitrage-style strategies exhibit high Sharpe ratios even under nontrivial trading costs. The edge persists because crypto remains segmented and structurally weird; it can vanish quickly when access widens, regulation changes, ETFs alter basis dynamics, or venues fail.

**Quantamental hybrids -- useful when disciplined, dangerous when they become narrative override.** The strongest hybrid approach is to use systematic priors--trend, carry, value, revisions, regime state--as the default map, then let human or model-assisted interpretation decide whether the edge is especially strong, especially weak, or best expressed differently in the current context. AQR's integrated macro framing and Man's point-in-time regime work fit this mold. The bad version of "quantamental" is just story-telling with a spreadsheet costume. The good version uses judgment to condition or express known edges, not to waive evidence standards.

## 3. Current Best Practice in Human Research Teams

The public window into elite research teams is imperfect and partly marketing. Even so, the overlap across Man AHL, Bridgewater, Two Sigma, and AQR is striking enough to be informative. They all emphasize scientific process, robust data/technology, explicit challenge, and cumulative learning rather than one-shot cleverness. In other words, the modern best practice is not "have better opinions"; it is "run a better error-correcting institution."

In practical terms, strong teams do not start with blank-page brainstorming. They start with structured intake: macro and corporate calendars, price/volume and spread anomalies, revision data, positioning or flow information, relative-value monitors, and curated research streams. The job of the initial layer is to surface **material, instrument-mappable changes** in expectations, risk transfer, or market structure. Raw inbound information is not yet a trade; it is only an input into a hypothesis card. That distinction matters because most news is trivia wearing a necktie.

The next common feature is explicit challenge culture. Bridgewater states that good outcomes require accurate understanding of reality and that nobody has the right to hold a critical opinion without speaking up. SR 11-7 frames model risk management around "effective challenge" by objective, informed parties. Two Sigma's public materials make the same point in research language: design for disproof, celebrate negative results, and use hidden out-samples. Strong teams do not "debate for balance." They pressure-test the thesis until either it survives or it dies.

Their testing culture is similarly unromantic. Two Sigma describes using high-quality data, robust backtesting, sound validation, MVPs, and out-samples hidden from the researcher until the right moment. SR 11-7 requires conceptual soundness, ongoing monitoring, benchmarking, and outcomes analysis. In practice, the institutional lesson is simple: no serious backtest should begin until the hypothesis, mechanism, horizon, invalidators, and data plan are written down. Otherwise research turns into adaptive storytelling, which is just overfitting with better manners.

Strong teams also separate **signal validity** from **trade expression**. A macro or event thesis may be true but still badly expressed in the wrong instrument. Exchange materials make clear how index, sector, VIX, and short-dated options are used to express directional, relative-value, volatility, and event-specific views; that flexibility is useful, but it also introduces Greek, decay, and liquidity complexity. The best practice is to validate the informational edge first, then choose the simplest instrument that captures it. Social-media-derived signals, by contrast, deserve low prior weight: some studies find firm-level Twitter sentiment contains information around analyst actions and earnings, but ESMA finds links between social sentiment and excess returns in EU equities are transitory, cost-blind, and not durable beyond a few days.

Finally, strong teams decide capital allocation on **portfolio worthiness**, not just signal prettiness. A style or event may be empirically real and still not deserve risk budget because of capacity, crowding, implementation cost, beta contamination, or correlation to existing book exposures. That is why live trading evidence and cost studies matter. Frazzini, Israel, and Moskowitz show that some anomalies survive costs better than older estimates suggest, but Patton and Weller show that all-in implementation costs can wipe out momentum and materially erode value for typical mutual funds. Large-order evidence in Treasuries shows execution costs worsen with order size. Capital is earned only after the idea survives reality, not because the chart looked handsome.

## 4. Where AI Helps and Where It Fails

AI adds real value first in **coverage and triage**. LLM-based systems are good at reading large volumes of text, extracting entities, classifying event types, summarizing research notes, comparing narratives across sources, and turning unstructured text into structured event cards. ESMA and the Alan Turing Institute note that current finance use cases center heavily on text analysis and production, while practitioners at Two Sigma describe AI as accelerating workflows and automating mundane tasks. That is the right use case: let the machine widen coverage and reduce clerical drag.

AI also adds value in **synthesis and challenge**, especially when modularized. ESMA/Turing point to RAG and fact-checking mechanisms as ways to reduce hallucinations and improve grounding, and they note that different models can validate each other. Man's AlphaTrend is more interesting than generic agent chatter because it uses a predefined, modular workflow, parallel queries for diversity, and consistency checks to spot contradictions. That is a good institutional pattern: use AI to generate bounded variants, retrieve prior evidence, and produce red-team objections--but do it inside typed workflows rather than in an unbounded conversation swamp.

AI is also useful in **research operations**: code scaffolding, experiment documentation, unit-test generation, monitor generation, and post-mortem drafting. Two Sigma's public comments are basically right here: AI can improve productivity and free researchers to focus on higher-level problems, and the competitive edge shifts from raw model power to integration, workflow design, and institutional judgment. That said, code generated by LLMs can introduce security risks if not audited, so generated research code should never be trusted like a house-trained saint. It should be treated like an eager intern with root access fantasies.

Now the dangerous half. AI is a **multiple-testing accelerant**. Two Sigma explicitly warns that agents can generate many hypotheses and backtests, worsening overfitting; they also warn that pretrained LLMs can "know" about later regime shifts and contaminate historical evaluation. The broader literature makes the same point from different angles: DSR exists because backtests are inflated by selection bias; PBO exists because optimal in-sample strategies often underperform out of sample; and multi-signal selection can create grotesque false confidence. So the main AI risk is not that it will miss all good ideas. It is that it will generate far too many plausible bad ones.

AI is also unreliable in the ways that matter most in high-stakes workflows: robustness, reproducibility, explainability, and accountability. ESMA/Turing explicitly frame responsible adoption around robustness, data dependency, security/privacy, fairness, and accountability; the Bank of England warns that complex AI models create predictability, explainability, transparency, data-integrity, and concentration risks; and it notes that more autonomous trading systems could take positions humans do not properly understand or ignore legal guardrails. That is not anti-technology pessimism. It is boring, correct institutional paranoia.

AI on **news and social media** deserves especially careful handling. News analytics can accelerate price and volume response, but inaccurate analytics create price distortions that are corrected quickly. Some academic work finds firm-level Twitter sentiment contains real information around analyst actions and earnings, but ESMA finds social-media-related excess returns are short-lived, ignore transaction costs, and show no longer-term relation. So AI should use news and X mainly as **attention and routing systems**: detect something changed, map it to instruments, and prompt a hypothesis. It should not leap from "the feed sounded bullish" to "deploy capital," which is how one ends up donating money to the market in a very modern format.

The practical rule is therefore asymmetric. Let AI be creatively divergent **upstream** and brutally constrained **downstream**. Use it to reduce false negatives in coverage and to improve challenge quality. Do not use it to lower the burden of proof for capital deployment. The more creative the model is, the tighter the experimental governance needs to be.

## 5. Recommended AI-Agent Team for BoxRoomCapital

Your initial instinct--wild idea generator, skeptic, synthesizer, portfolio boss--captures only the debate layer. It misses the institutional plumbing that actually protects capital: provenance, experimental design, implementation realism, model validation, and retirement discipline. The optimal design is function-first, not personality-first; and the final adjudicator should be human, not another model wearing a fake moustache of authority.

**1) Source Reliability & Materiality Agent.** Mandate: convert raw news, X posts, website posts, notes, and market snapshots into canonical event cards with timestamps, source class, corroboration status, affected instruments, and materiality score. Strength: high-volume coverage and provenance discipline. Failure mode: laundering rumor into "fact," especially from social media or low-quality repost chains. Decision rights: no trade or hypothesis authority. Inputs: whitelisted feeds plus raw metadata. Outputs: `EventCard`. Handoff: only corroborated, instrument-mapped events proceed.

**2) Market State & Regime Agent.** Mandate: maintain a point-in-time state vector for macro, liquidity, volatility, trend, carry environment, and existing book context. Strength: keeps hypotheses anchored to "what regime are we probably in?" rather than "what story did we just hear?" Failure mode: seductive but spurious regime labeling or look-ahead bias via variable selection. Decision rights: no trades; provides conditioning context and failure-regime history. Inputs: market data snapshots and approved state variables. Outputs: `StateSnapshot`. Handoff: to hypothesis, portfolio, and monitor agents.

**3) Hypothesis & Divergence Agent.** Mandate: generate a small number of candidate hypotheses, but only within an approved edge taxonomy: underreaction/revision, carry/risk transfer, flow/positioning, relative value, convexity/insurance, or regime dislocation. Strength: creative recombination of known mechanisms. Failure mode: novelty-seeking nonsense, style drift, or producing dozens of variants that silently expand the search space. Decision rights: may propose; may not promote. Inputs: `EventCard`, `StateSnapshot`, and edge-taxonomy rules. Outputs: `HypothesisCard` with mechanism, direction, horizon, catalyst, invalidators, and candidate instruments.

**4) Mechanism & Literature Agent.** Mandate: retrieve prior empirical evidence, practitioner analogs, known failure regimes, crowding history, and implementation caveats for each hypothesis. Strength: stops the system from reinventing old factors and mistaking familiarity for novelty. Failure mode: cherry-picking papers or treating practitioner marketing as proof. Decision rights: can downgrade or recommend parking if prior evidence is weak or already crowded. Inputs: `HypothesisCard` and research memory. Outputs: `EvidenceDossier`.

**5) Red-Team Falsification Agent.** Mandate: assume the thesis is false and identify the cheapest alternative explanation. Strength: omitted-variable hunting, beta leakage detection, crowding checks, legal/compliance objections, and "is this just short vol in disguise?" analysis. Failure mode: nihilism that kills everything, or lazy criticism that is stylistic rather than causal. Decision rights: can veto promotion until objections are answered, but cannot approve live deployment. Inputs: `HypothesisCard`, `EvidenceDossier`, and `StateSnapshot`. Outputs: `FalsificationMemo`.

**6) Experiment Registrar & Data Integrity Agent.** Mandate: freeze the hypothesis ID, point-in-time data sources, baselines, train/validation/test splits, search budget, and all attempted variants before any backtest runs. Strength: prevents silent degree-of-freedom creep. Failure mode: bureaucratic rigidity or incomplete logging. Decision rights: no registered spec, no backtest. Inputs: hypothesis package and approved datasets. Outputs: `TestSpec`.

**7) Backtest & Cost-Reality Agent.** Mandate: run simulations under all-in cost, capacity, and liquidity assumptions; compute DSR/PBO-style robustness metrics; stress subsamples and failure regimes. Strength: translates pretty theory into ugly reality. Failure mode: under-modeling slippage, borrow, funding, roll, option-surface friction, or venue risk. Decision rights: may recommend `promote`, `revise`, `park`, or `reject`; may not deploy. Inputs: `TestSpec`, market data, cost models. Outputs: `ExperimentReport`.

**8) Portfolio Expression & Risk Agent.** Mandate: choose the cleanest trade expression--cash, futures, spread, options overlay, relative-value pair--and propose sizing, stop/exit logic, hedge plan, and expected interaction with existing exposures. Strength: separates "signal is real" from "this is the right instrument." Failure mode: overengineering, hidden short-gamma/short-vol exposure, or mismatch between thesis horizon and instrument decay. Decision rights: can simplify or reject a trade construction even if the underlying signal is valid. Inputs: `ExperimentReport`, `StateSnapshot`, live book context. Outputs: `TradeSheet`.

**9) Deployment & Model-Risk Gate Agent.** Mandate: act like internal model risk. Check conceptual soundness, validation completeness, documentation, versioning, model inventory, approval status, and kill-rule readiness. Strength: institutional memory and procedural discipline. Failure mode: becoming a checkbox gremlin detached from economics. Decision rights: can pass only to human sign-off, or send back for revision/parking. Inputs: full research packet plus version metadata. Outputs: `ApprovalPacket`.

**10) Live Monitoring & Retirement Agent.** Mandate: watch signal health, realized slippage, exposure drift, crowding proxies, data integrity, regime mismatch, and thesis invalidators; separate "data broke" from "edge broke." Strength: ongoing surveillance and disciplined retirement. Failure mode: alert fatigue or slow recognition of decay. Decision rights: may auto-pause only within preauthorized micro-limits; may never auto-scale up. Inputs: live fills, market data, thesis monitors, data-quality telemetry. Outputs: `HealthReport`, `RetirementMemo`.

**11) Human CIO / Risk Owner.** Mandate: final authority over capital deployment, exception handling, kill decisions, and any change to risk limits or operating mandate. Strength: accountability, judgment under ambiguity, and responsibility for model misuse. Failure mode: overruling the process because a story feels compelling. Decision rights: final yes/no. Inputs: `ApprovalPacket` and live-risk context. Outputs: deployment approval or rejection.

Two design rules matter more than the agent count. First, there should be **no democracy** among agents; majority vote is a lousy substitute for burden of proof. Second, the creative agent should have **zero capital authority**. The right asymmetry is this: one serious unresolved objection should block promotion, while five enthusiastic AI voices should not force a trade through. Promote only when objections are answered, revise when the mechanism is plausible but the test or expression is weak, park when the idea is economically sensible but mistimed or data-poor, and reject when the thesis is narrative-only, contaminated, or unimplementable.

## 6. Recommended End-to-End Workflow

**Stage 0: Define the mandate before the machine sees a headline.** Fix the initial universe, instrument whitelist, leverage rules, and approved edge taxonomy. Without a fixed mandate, every new source becomes an excuse to expand scope, and the system turns into a cross-asset improv troupe. For MVP, the universe should be small and liquid.

**Stage 1: Intake and normalization.** Ingest news, X posts, website updates, research notes, and scheduled market snapshots. Deduplicate, timestamp, classify source type, map named entities to instruments, and preserve raw provenance. No summarization should overwrite the raw record.

**Stage 2: Reliability and materiality triage.** Score the source for trustworthiness, corroboration, and likely market relevance. Official filings, central-bank releases, exchange notices, and primary transcripts get high prior weight; social posts get low prior weight unless official or rapidly corroborated. Most inbound items should die here. That is healthy.

**Stage 3: Signal extraction.** Convert material items into structured observations: what changed, for whom, with what magnitude, versus which likely market expectation. This stage should also pull the current state/regime vector so the system knows whether the observation lands in a friendly or hostile environment. The output is still not a trade; it is a candidate discrepancy between market view and reality.

**Stage 4: Hypothesis formation.** Produce a `HypothesisCard` with the fields: edge family, observation, market-implied view, proposed variant view, causal chain, candidate direction, horizon, catalyst, invalidators, likely failure regimes, and candidate expressions. Limit to a handful of variants. More variants are not "thoroughness"; they are unlogged multiple testing in a trench coat.

**Stage 5: Falsification and challenge.** Run a separate adversarial pass that searches for alternative explanations, beta leakage, crowding, hidden short-vol, data quality problems, legal issues, and implementation traps. A hypothesis that cannot survive a hostile reading should never touch a backtest, because the backtest will happily flatter nonsense if you ask nicely enough.

**Stage 6: Regime-fit assessment.** Compare the thesis against similar historical states, known crash regimes, and structural break risks. Use point-in-time regime mapping as a contextual tool, not as a mystical oracle, and explicitly log where variable choices might induce look-ahead bias. Regime assessment can block or park a thesis even if the mechanism is real.

**Stage 7: Test registration.** Before any backtest, freeze the data sources, point-in-time availability rules, train/validation/test partitions, baseline models, hyperparameter budget, and variant count. If the team exceeds the search budget, the hypothesis should be reclassified as a new hypothesis family with harsher evidentiary standards. This is how you stop the overfitting factory from building its own shrine.

**Stage 8: Backtest and implementation realism.** Run the test with asset-class-specific cost models: spreads, commissions, market impact, borrow and locate for equities, roll/slippage for futures, vol-surface and legging risk for options, funding and margin for crypto, and venue/liquidity constraints everywhere. Report gross and net results, stability across subsamples, DSR/PBO-style diagnostics, and comparison to simple baselines. A beautiful gross Sharpe with bad live economics is not a strategy; it is a screenplay.

**Stage 9: Portfolio-fit and expression review.** Separate the informational edge from the chosen instrument. Decide whether the thesis belongs in futures, cash, a spread, or an options overlay; estimate how it interacts with current book exposures; and ensure the chosen structure does not introduce unintended tail risks or timing mismatch. Many good ideas should be rejected or simplified at this stage.

**Stage 10: Forward test / shadow book.** No strategy should jump directly from backtest to meaningful live capital. First run it in paper or micro-size, logging fill quality, latency tolerance, signal stability, and monitor behavior. Two Sigma's MVP and hidden-outsample discipline is the right spirit here: small, measurable proof before commitment.

**Stage 11: Risk approval and controlled deployment.** Require the full packet: hypothesis, falsification memo, registered test, experiment report, trade sheet, kill rules, model/prompt versions, and sign-off. Deployment should follow a capital ladder: paper, micro, pilot, then scale--never the reverse. Early automation may reduce size automatically under prewritten conditions, but auto-scaling up should be prohibited.

**Stage 12: Live monitoring and degradation detection.** Monitor four things separately: alpha health, implementation health, data health, and control health. Examples: rolling information content, realized slippage versus modeled slippage, exposure drift, crowding proxies, borrow/funding changes, source outages, and violation of thesis invalidators. Degradation should trigger `revise`, `park`, or `retire`; not "let's wait and see because the model feels misunderstood."

**Stage 13: Kill criteria and retirement.** Hard-kill if there is a data-provenance breach, control failure, unexplained model behavior, or legal/compliance issue. Thesis-kill if the explicit invalidator occurs or if live performance and execution deviate materially and persistently from the registered expectation set. Park--not kill--if the edge is still plausible but current regime or capacity conditions are hostile. Archive every retirement memo, because dead ideas are part of the knowledge base.

The workflow outcome at each gate should be only one of four states: `promote`, `revise`, `park`, or `reject`. Promote when the mechanism is clear, point-in-time testable, net of realistic costs, and not obviously redundant to existing book exposures. Revise when the economic story survives but the test design or trade expression is weak. Park when the idea is plausible but mistimed, data-poor, or outside current mandate. Reject when it is narrative-only, contaminated, implementation-infeasible, or fails challenge. That state discipline matters because a research queue without parking and rejection becomes a hoarder's attic with Sharpe ratios.

## 7. Governance and Risk Controls

The first governance rule is blunt: **no fully autonomous capital allocation in MVP**. The Bank of England explicitly warns that more autonomous AI trading could create positions not well understood by human risk managers, could ignore legal guardrails, and could lead to correlated positioning across firms. FCA guidance emphasizes accountability for senior managers, and ESMA/Turing stresses human-in-the-loop accountability frameworks. For BoxRoomCapital, this means the human CIO/risk owner must approve every initial deployment, every capital increase, and every override.

The second rule is **full auditability**. SR 11-7 requires detailed documentation of development, validation, assumptions, limitations, and an inventory of models in use or retired. NIST's AI RMF and GenAI profile emphasize managing AI risk through design, evaluation, and trustworthiness. For BoxRoomCapital, prompts, model versions, retrieval settings, code hashes, datasets, search budgets, test results, approvals, and live changes should all be versioned and immutable in the audit trail. A prompt change that materially alters behavior is a model change, not just a spicy edit.

The third rule is **separation of research and deployment**. Research agents should never have broker credentials. The execution path should only accept approved `TradeSheet` objects signed by the deployment gate and human risk owner. Vendor and model dependencies should be explicit, because ESMA and the Bank of England both highlight third-party concentration and infrastructure dependence as real risks. If a vendor model or data source changes materially, the affected strategies should move back into review rather than gliding forward on stale trust.

The fourth rule is **evidence standards above narrative standards**. Require conceptual soundness, point-in-time data, realistic costs, hidden or genuinely untouched evaluation data, robustness across subsamples, and explicit attention to search-space inflation. In practice, raw Sharpe and "the backtest looked robust" are insufficient. Use stronger statistical hurdles, DSR/PBO-style diagnostics, and explicit search-budget accounting. A thesis that survives these screens still may not be deployable, but a thesis that does not survive them should be euthanized without ceremony.

The fifth rule is **capital controls that assume error**. Use approved instrument lists, hard notional and leverage caps, per-strategy loss limits, market-specific liquidity caps, manual kill switches, and restricted hours or venues where needed. Early strategies should be allowed to auto-throttle down within prewritten limits but never auto-scale up. That is consistent with both model-risk discipline and the regulatory emphasis on accountability and controlled use of AI in consequential settings.

The sixth rule is about the one-person problem. A one-person shop cannot truly achieve independence of challenge the way a large institution can. The minimum compensating controls are: temporal separation between research and approval, mandatory checklists, locked memos before tests are opened, periodic external review of the process, and refusal to let the same context window both generate and approve a strategy. It is not perfect independence. It is simply much better than trusting one mind--human or machine--to mark its own homework.

## 8. Build Blueprint for the Dev Team

**System architecture.** Build BoxRoomCapital as a typed research operating system with six layers: an ingestion layer for news/X/websites/notes/snapshots; a canonical event store preserving raw provenance; a research memory layer indexing literature and prior experiments; an experiment registry with point-in-time dataset rules and search-budget logging; a backtest/cost/portfolio engine; and a deployment-monitoring layer with approvals, model inventory, and live telemetry. Man's modular agentic workflow is the right architectural inspiration here because it is predefined, reproducible, transparent, and auditable, which aligns neatly with SR 11-7 and NIST-style governance.

**Recommended agent roster and persona briefs.**
Source Reliability Agent: default posture is distrust; it upgrades only corroborated facts.
State/Regime Agent: default posture is contextual; it asks "in what environment would this work or fail?"
Hypothesis Agent: default posture is constrained divergence; novelty gets low weight unless the mechanism is strong.
Mechanism/Literature Agent: default posture is historical memory; it asks "is this actually new, and what killed it before?"
Red-Team Agent: default posture is hostile falsification; it searches for the cheapest reason the thesis is wrong.
Experiment Registrar: default posture is methodological policing; every trial counts.
Backtest/Cost Agent: default posture is implementation realism; gross returns are suspect until netted and stressed.
Portfolio Agent: default posture is simplification; the simplest instrument that captures the thesis usually wins.
Deployment/Model-Risk Agent: default posture is "not yet"; documentation and controls must be complete.
Live Monitor: default posture is decay detection; it distinguishes thesis failure from plumbing failure.

**Instruction principles for every agent.** Every output must explicitly separate `observation`, `inference`, and `recommendation`. Every output must state assumptions, confidence, source provenance, known failure modes, and what evidence would change the conclusion. Every agent must prefer "no decision" to forced output. Agents may not cite their own earlier prose as evidence; they must cite raw inputs or archived experiment artifacts. Material prompt or model changes increment version numbers and can invalidate downstream approvals.

**Canonical artifact schemas.**
`EventCard`: source IDs, source class, timestamp, corroboration count, claims extracted, affected instruments, novelty, materiality, credibility.
`HypothesisCard`: hypothesis ID, edge family, market-implied view, variant view, mechanism, catalyst, horizon, direction, invalidators, known failure regimes, candidate expressions.
`TestSpec`: point-in-time dataset definitions, feature list, train/validation/test splits, baseline models, search budget, cost model, evaluation metrics.
`ExperimentReport`: gross/net performance, DSR/PBO-style diagnostics, robustness checks, capacity, correlation, implementation caveats.
`TradeSheet`: instrument(s), sizing logic, entry/exit, expected holding period, hedge plan, risk limits, monitor metrics, kill criteria.
`RetirementMemo`: trigger, diagnosis, lessons, whether the idea is dead or merely parked.

**Skill definitions.** The development team should implement skills as deterministic or constrained services, not free-form "agent powers." Core skills: `dedupe_and_entity_link`, `source_reliability_score`, `event_to_hypothesis`, `retrieve_literature_and_priors`, `generate_falsifiers`, `register_experiment`, `build_point_in_time_dataset`, `run_backtest_with_costs`, `estimate_capacity_and_liquidity`, `design_trade_expression`, `generate_monitor_profile`, and `archive_audit_record`. Retrieval-based skills should use trusted corpora and preserve citations to original evidence.

**Handoff rules.** No free-form agent chat loops in production workflow. Handoffs must be structured objects plus attached evidence references. An agent may only consume fields that the previous gate explicitly released. Unresolved red-team objections must travel with the artifact; they may not be silently dropped by a synthesizer. This is one place where your original "balanced synthesizer" instinct should be demoted: a smoothing agent is exactly how organizations accidentally sand off the sharp edges of risk.

**Scoring rubric.** Use a 100-point score before penalties. Source integrity 10; mechanism clarity 15; prior empirical support 15; incremental information advantage 10; regime fit 10; point-in-time testability 10; implementation realism/costs/capacity 15; portfolio fit 10; monitoring/kill clarity 5. Then apply penalties: search-space/complexity up to -15, crowding up to -10, and vendor/data fragility up to -10. Default thresholds: below 60 reject; 60-69 revise or park; 70-79 eligible for registered testing; 80-89 eligible for paper or micro pilot if robustness passes; 90+ eligible for live pilot only with human sign-off and no unresolved red-team objection. The weights are design choices, but the principle--low novelty weight, high implementation weight--is non-negotiable.

**Daily / intraday operating cadence.** Pre-open: refresh state/regime snapshot, overnight event cards, live-book health, and scheduled release calendar. Intraday: only event-triggered workflows on material releases, major source updates, or monitor breaches. Post-close: literature retrieval, deeper hypothesis work, registered tests, and red-team review. Weekly: promotion/parking/retirement committee with locked artifacts. Monthly: model inventory review, vendor drift review, prompt/version review, and cost-model recalibration. The cadence should be calm by default and fast only by exception. That is how you stop the system from interpreting every market hiccup as a call to heroism.

**MVP design.** Start with a deliberately narrow mandate. Recommended production candidates are:

1. liquid cross-asset futures playbooks centered on known style premia such as trend and carry, conditioned by regime/state;
2. US large-cap event/revision playbooks anchored in underreaction and earnings/news interpretation.
   Use options mainly as expression overlays after the underlying thesis is validated. Restrict sources to primary releases, earnings transcripts, major news wires, curated research notes, and whitelisted official X accounts. Exclude autonomous OTC credit, vol-specialist premium harvesting, high-frequency stat arb, and broad crypto deployment from MVP.

**Advanced version.** After the system proves it can reject weak ideas, add more complex layers in this order: richer regime conditioning, more sophisticated relative-value across futures and ETFs, options-based expression templates, selected credit index or ETF proxy playbooks, and only then tightly controlled crypto basis/perpetual strategies on approved venues. The criterion for expansion is not "we built more agents." It is "the existing process demonstrates stable live discipline and low false-positive rates."

## 9. Final Recommendation

Build BoxRoomCapital first as a **research and validation operating system**, not as a fully autonomous hedge fund. The mission of the first version is not to generate maximum idea count. It is to generate fewer, better, more falsifiable ideas and to stop bad ideas from reaching capital. In institutional terms, success is a lower false-positive rate, cleaner auditability, and better implementation realism--not more animated agent banter.

The best starting scope is a narrow set of liquid markets and old, durable edge families with strong public priors: trend/carry/regime-conditioned macro in liquid futures, and event/revision underreaction in large-cap equities. Use AI heavily for coverage, synthesis, literature retrieval, challenge, and monitoring. Use it lightly for creative divergence. Do not let it allocate meaningful capital autonomously. The seasoned adjudicator in your original idea should be a human risk owner, and the "balanced synthesizer" should mostly be replaced by typed artifacts and hard gates.

In plainer language: build a machine that behaves like a stern head of research, not like a caffeinated junior PM with a browser and a dream. The public evidence favors disciplined harvesting of known frictions over grandiose novelty. BoxRoomCapital should therefore optimize for **truth, rejection quality, and execution realism**. Profit, to the extent it comes, will come from that discipline rather than from theatrical AI creativity.

## 10. Red Team: Why This Entire Plan Might Still Fail

The most brutal objection is that BoxRoomCapital may have **no durable informational advantage at all**. If the system mostly reads public news, public research, public social media, and standard market data with general-purpose models, it may simply reproduce consensus more efficiently. Regulators already note that many firms use general-purpose models and common third-party providers, while the Bank of England warns that widespread use of similar models and data could lead to correlated positioning. In that world, the edge is not just thin; it is commoditized before it is born.

A second objection is that even a well-designed agent system can still become an **overfitting factory with nice documentation**. Agents make idea generation cheaper, which encourages more trials, which increases false discoveries unless the search budget is policed ruthlessly. Two Sigma explicitly warns about this; DSR, PBO, and multi-signal backtest bias all say the same thing in colder language. The illusion of edge is most likely in any workflow that rewards idea count, lets agents iterate silently, or treats backtest improvement as evidence rather than as something to distrust.

A third objection is that some proposed agent roles may create more noise than value. The most dangerous is the unconstrained "out-of-the-box idea generator," because it expands the search space faster than validation can control it. The next worst is the "balanced synthesizer," because it tends to smooth away unresolved objections and convert real uncertainty into coherent prose. A third candidate for simplification is any social-media interpreter agent that tries to convert noisy online chatter directly into trade views. Social inputs can be useful as attention radars, but ESMA's evidence suggests their return effects are short-lived and cost-blind.

A fourth objection is **execution reality**. Many historically real effects either weaken materially after all-in costs or require infrastructure, balance sheet, borrow access, dealer relationships, venue management, or options expertise that a one-person shop will not have. This is especially true for OTC credit, specialist vol trading, and parts of crypto. A skeptical CIO would say: "Your research architecture is more sophisticated than your execution moat." That is not a small problem. It is the whole game.

A fifth objection is **governance insufficiency in a one-person setup**. SR 11-7 wants effective challenge and some independence in validation. FCA and ESMA stress accountability; the Bank of England stresses autonomy risks. A solo operator can emulate separation of duties, but cannot fully replicate institutional independence. A skeptical CIO would therefore object that the plan still relies too heavily on the discipline of one human and a set of vendor models. That may be acceptable for a research lab. It is a much shakier foundation for scalable capital.

The simplification I would make before implementation is severe. Cut the market scope. Cut the number of autonomous agents. Remove free-form debate loops. Start with liquid futures and large-cap equities only. Make options expression-only at first. Ban autonomous deployment. Whitelist sources aggressively. Treat every material prompt or model change as a revalidation event. Add external review sooner than feels convenient. A skeptical CIO would rather fund a smaller, duller, falsifiable machine than a grand AI contraption that confuses architecture with edge. The universe does not pay for impressive diagrams. It pays, occasionally and grudgingly, for disciplined contact with reality.
